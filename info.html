Vincent Van Gogh

Self-Portrait (1889) This painting is also one of his many works that were created in the Saint-Paul asylum. The intense, swirling background contrasts with his calm yet deeply introspective expression. The use of bold colors—greens, blues, and reds—highlights his signature style of expressing emotion through color, rather than pure realism. Interestingly, this is one of several self-portraits Van Gogh painted, using himself as a model when he couldn’t afford others. His gaze, slightly off-center, seems to reflect both strength and vulnerability. This portrait is often seen as one of Van Gogh’s most personal works, capturing the emotional depth he experienced during his struggles with mental illness.

Starry Night

Starry Night (1889) Perhaps Van Gogh’s most famous work, he painted Starry Night during his stay at the Saint-Paul asylum. What’s fascinating is that the swirling night sky wasn’t just imagined—Vincent painted it from memory, inspired by the view from his window. The swirling sky, glowing stars, and bold strokes capture movement and energy, reflecting his fascination with nature's forces. Despite its dreamlike beauty, Starry Night reflects Van Gogh’s inner turmoil. The dark cypress tree, symbolizing both death and hope, cuts through the scene, while the village below remains still and quiet—a contrast to the sky’s wild energy. Surprisingly, Van Gogh himself wasn’t a fan of the painting at first, calling it a "failure." Yet today, it’s one of the most celebrated artworks in the world.

Café Terrace at Night

Café Terrace at Night (1888) Painted during Van Gogh’s stay in Arles, Café Terrace at Night captures a vibrant evening scene at a café in the Place du Forum. The warm glow of the café’s lights contrasts with the deep blues of the night sky, creating a sense of intimacy and mystery. What’s unique about this painting is how Van Gogh experiments with color and light, showing the night without using black—a bold choice at the time. This piece reflects Van Gogh’s growing fascination with the emotional power of color, a shift that would define many of his later works. It’s also one of the first paintings where he experimented with his famous "starry" night sky, which would soon lead to his iconic Starry Night.

Bedroom in Arles

Bedroom in Arles (1888) Bedroom in Arles offers a glimpse into Van Gogh’s personal world, depicting his simple room in the Yellow House in Arles. The vivid reds, blues, and yellows create a sense of calm yet slight tension, reflecting both the comfort and loneliness Vincent felt at the time. What makes this painting stand out is Van Gogh’s use of color to express emotion rather than realism—the tilted perspective and flatness of the objects add to the dreamlike quality. It’s an intimate portrayal of a space that was both a retreat and a reflection of his inner struggles.

Undergrowth with two figures

Undergrowth with two figures (1890) In the spring of 1890, just months before his death, Vincent left the South to be near his brother Theo’s family in Paris. He found the city too loud and felt his presence might disrupt their new life, so he settled in Auvers-sur-Oise, about 30 minutes outside the city, under the care of a local doctor. During the summer, Vincent painted the picturesque landscapes around Auvers, trudging out into the countryside every day. These final works, including Undergrowth with Two Figures, showcase some of his most beautiful and poignant creations, reflecting his emotional intensity during this challenging time.

First Steps

First Steps (January 1890) Struggles with his mental health led Van Gogh to admit himself to the psychiatric hospital of Saint-Rémy-de-Provence, located in the Romanesque monastery of Saint-Paul-de-Mausole. During his stay at the hospital, Vincent went through cycles of recovery and relapse. When he couldn't go outside to paint, he kept busy by reproducing works from other artists. First Step is one such example, a reimagining of Jean-François Millet’s 1858 painting. Though it's a reproduction, Vincent’s unique style and vision shine through, giving new life to a classic.

Vase with Red & White Carnations on a Yellow Background

Vase with Red & White Carnations on a Yellow Background (1886) In Paris, Vincent had a tough time finding models—either no one wanted to pose, or he couldn’t afford them. When his usual sources, like Parisian brothels, fell through, he turned to still lifes: flowers, books, plaster busts, and landscapes became his go-to subjects. Vase with Red & White Carnations on a Yellow Background is one example. While earthy tones dominate, those bright red carnations hint at his upcoming obsession with color. Paris wasn’t easy on Van Gogh. The local art scene shrugged him off, calling his self-taught style amateurish. Despite his brother Theo's connections, no one cared about his work. By the end, things got so bad that models refused to work with him, and even painting in public led to harassment from the police. Theo summed it up well: people either loved Vincent or found him impossible to deal with.

Autumn Landscape

Autumn Landscape (October 1885) During his tough time in Neuen, Van Gogh painted moody, dark autumn landscapes, sticking to black, gray, and brown tones. But after clashing with locals, he left for Antwerp in late 1885. Everything changed when he moved to Paris in 1886. Inspired by artists like Gauguin and Monet, Vincent started experimenting with color. You'll see this transformation if you compare his earlier gloomy works to his later vibrant ones. A quick tip: If Van Gogh’s painting looks dark and shadowy, it’s probably from before 1886. After that, his art lights up with color!
